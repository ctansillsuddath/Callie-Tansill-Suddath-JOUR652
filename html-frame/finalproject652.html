background info.

Of the eight emergency rooms that serve the D.C. public, only one — Medstar Washington Hospital Center — offers rape kit services.

The Situation

The issue runs deeper than the boundaries of a box. A “rape kit” describes only the physical package in which collected evidence is stored. It is the number of nurses qualified to conduct the forensic exam necessary for collecting a rape kit that complicates things. In the aftermath of a sexual assault, a survivor may choose to obtain DNA information about their assailant. This is done for a number of reasons, including in preparation to pursue a criminal case. For survivors in Washington, D.C., this process is complicated by minimal forensic services.

Forensic nurses complete extensive, specialized training before they are able to conduct an exam. In addition to a year of experience working in critical care, nurses complete 45 hours of classroom instruction, and participate in a number of “hands-on” exercises. When a patient presents at the Medstar ER seeking care after an assault, they can rely on being seen by a forensic nurse examiner after undergoing the initial emergency department triage. Medstar even has a separate, private room — complete with a door rather than a curtain — solely used for this purpose. The program at Medstar Washington Hospital Center is a partnership between the hospital and the non-profit organization, District of Columbia Forensic Nurse Examiners. But, it is the sole program in the city. Why don’t other area hospitals just train and keep their own SANE nurses on hand? A few factors prevent this. >

The nature of the exam, Colleen Gallopin of the District of Columbia Forensic Nurse Examiners explains, largely explains this. A forensic exam is more detailed than your run-of-the-mill check up, and forensic nurses know to look for things others may miss. “Our nurse assess[es] anything that is an injury, pain, tenderness, and then documents it on a body map,” she explains. “We’ll do a pelvic exam. We’ll do photographs of any injuries, and then take down patient documentation of their report, as certain things are not photographable— pain, for example. Then, based on the patient’s account of what occurred, we will do the physical evidence collection, or the kit.” The exam alone could take upwards of four hours, and does not end when every stretch of skin has been seen. Body parts are swabbed, clothing is collected, and when appropriate, toxicology samples are taken. HIV testing, prophylaxis, and pregnancy prevention are also provided when necessary.
